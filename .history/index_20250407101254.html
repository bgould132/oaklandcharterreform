Why Charter Reform?

"Oakland's government is designed to fail."

Every stakeholder we've met with – many with decades of experience working for or in partnership with the Oakland city government – told us that Oakland's flawed city charter is either partly or largely responsible for the City's dysfunction.

The recent recall of Mayor Sheng Thao represents a peak moment of dissatisfaction with Oakland's government. No other mayor in Oakland's 173-year history has ever been recalled from office. An astonishing 79% of Oakland residents feel the City is on the wrong track.

Oakland's "hybrid structure makes it difficult to establish accountability for the effective functioning of government since it leaves open the question of which branch of government is in charge." In other words - the buck stops nowhere.

Our Principles

Accountability

Clear lines of responsibility so the buck stops somewhere definite, enabling residents to hold their government accountable.

Effectiveness

A government structure that enables efficient delivery of services and implementation of policies to improve quality of life.

Representation

Ensuring all Oakland residents have their voices heard and interests represented in city government.

Transparency

Open and accessible government processes that allow residents to understand how decisions are made.

Our Approach

The Oakland Charter Reform Project has conducted extensive research and community engagement to inform our approach to charter reform:

  • Three focus groups with diverse Oakland stakeholders
  • One-on-one interviews with community members, workers, business leaders, elected officials, and more
  • Special information session with Oakland NAACP leaders
  • Analysis of charter structures in comparable cities

We are exploring options for placing a charter reform measure before voters in June 2026. Why June? Because settling matters about the City's organization in advance of the November 2026 mayoral election will allow candidates to run for a clearly defined role in the new system.

Proposed Amendments

Based on extensive research and stakeholder input, we are exploring the following potential amendments to the Oakland City Charter:

Governance Structure

Oakland's hybrid governance structure has created confusion about who is responsible for what. We propose moving to a clear governance structure where lines of authority and accountability are well-defined:

  • Put the Mayor in charge of Council: The Mayor's disconnection from Council is a source of dysfunction. It drives status competitions between the two bodies, diminishing service for Oaklanders. It leaves the Mayor less effective and unable to deliver on their policy priorities. Making Oakland's Mayor the chair of the Council would give the Mayor a strong voice in Council decisions, and help ensure that the Council is responsive to the Mayor's agenda.
  • Council-Manager System: Reverting to a professional city manager government where the city council serves as the board of directors with a mayor acting as council chair.

City Attorney Appointment

Currently, Oakland's city attorney is independently elected, creating potential conflicts between serving voters' interests and the municipal corporation's needs. We propose:

  • Changing to an appointed city attorney who is hired by and answers to the City Council
  • Ensuring the city attorney views the City Council and city manager as their client

Commission Effectiveness

Reform Oakland's commissions to improve transparency, responsiveness, effectiveness, efficiency, and equity outcomes.

Financial Guardrails

Establish an independent controller who provides real-time monitoring and reporting on expenditures, revenues, and contract adherence.
